1. Mafia — who, where, and what it means for a tourist

Four historical mafia organizations operate in Italy. Each has its own territory, structure and methods. Here's their current map — and, importantly, what it really means for an ordinary tourist.

Main groups and territories

'Ndrangheta

Origin: Calabria (south, the "toe of the boot").

Power: today the richest and most powerful of all. Globally dominates cocaine trade into Europe.

Expansion: active in north-west Italy (Lombardy, Piedmont) and abroad (Germany, Netherlands, Australia, Canada).

Cosa Nostra

Origin: Sicily.

Power: historically most famous, today weakened by state pressure. February 2025 saw the largest raid since 1984 — nearly 150 arrests.

Fields: protection, public contracts, drugs.

Camorra

Origin: Campania (around Naples).

Structure: not unified — about 180 independent clans, often feuding.

Fields: drugs, counterfeit goods, waste management, restaurant protection.

Sacra Corona Unita

Origin: Apulia ("heel").

Power: youngest and smallest of the four.

Fields: smuggling (borders Albania), drugs.

Risk for tourists — honestly

For an ordinary tourist, the risk is practically zero. The mafia isn't interested in individual visitors — it targets business: hotels, restaurants, public contracts, infrastructure. According to Italian association Confcommercio, mafia extracts roughly €3.3 billion a year from the tourism sector, but in the form of protection payments from business owners, not by robbing tourists.

That means:

  • In a restaurant you pick as a tourist, you'll feel the mafia at most through prices. If a restaurant has long-term fixed prices near tourist landmarks and staff lure you in, locals often know and avoid it.
  • Violence between mafia and state yes, between mafia and tourist no. Cases of attacks on tourists in connection with organized crime are extremely rare.
  • What you actually deal with as a tourist: petty pickpockets, scammers near monuments, not reading the bill before paying. That's NOT mafia — those are individual thieves. See the Safety section.

Where to be careful as a driver / guide: in Calabria and some parts of Naples and Apulia, locals know which restaurants / suppliers it's better to avoid (due to likely protection). If you travel with a local guide, it pays to listen.

Sources: Organized crime in Italy (Wikipedia) · Fortune — Mafia & tourism (12/2024) · Europol Threat Assessment (PDF)

2. Italian customs — what's polite and what can cost you a fine

At the table

  • Pasta is not cut with a knife. Roll it with the fork.
  • No parmesan on fish pasta. Italians consider it a mistake. Cheese overwhelms the delicate fish flavor. Also no parmesan on seafood pasta.
  • Cappuccino is morning only (until ~11:00, max 12:00). Not after lunch or dinner. Italians believe milk disrupts digestion after food. Macchiato is OK any time.
  • "Fare la scarpetta" — mopping up sauce with bread. In informal settings absolutely fine, often even expected.
  • Coperto: in restaurants you're usually charged €2–4 per person as "cover" / bread & seat fee. Not a tip — it's a standard line item.
  • Tipping: not mandatory. If the bill has servizio 10%, you don't add anything. Otherwise round up, max 5–10%. Never 15–20% like in the US.

Greetings and address

  • Buongiorno — good day (until ~14:00–15:00)
  • Buonasera — good afternoon / good evening
  • Salve — neutral "hello" (more formal than ciao)
  • Ciao — hi (only among friends, NOT in shops / restaurants)
  • Between friends (especially women) kiss on both cheeks (right first, then left). Between men usually a handshake.

Clothing

  • Churches and basilicas: shoulders and knees covered. No exceptions. St Peter's in the Vatican turns away anyone improperly dressed.
  • In centers of some towns (Capri, Sorrento, Portofino) there are bans on swimwear and bare torso outside the beach. Fine up to €500.

Beach — rules and fines 2025–2026

Italy has tightened rules especially in Sardinia. Watch out for:

  • Taking sand or pebbles as a souvenir from Sardinia: illegal. Fines €500–3,000, in extreme cases theft of public property — risk of prison. Customs at Cagliari and Olbia airports check luggage.
  • La Pelosa (Sardinia): towel DIRECTLY on sand forbidden. You must have a mat (straw/bamboo), towel on top. €100 fine on the spot. Entry limited to 1,500 people/day, reservation via app.
  • Deep holes and big sandcastles (Sardinia): forbidden. Fine up to €3,000. Coastal ecological protection.
  • Capri 2026: new rules against overtourism, reservations required on some beaches.

Car and traffic

  • ZTL (Zona a Traffico Limitato): in centers of most historical towns car entry is forbidden. Camera gates photograph plates — fines €80–300, often arrive home half a year after the trip. If your hotel is inside the ZTL, the hotel arranges permission in advance.
  • Never drive into the centers of Florence, Rome, Bologna, Milan or Naples without precise navigation and ZTL check.
  • Vignette: none in Italy. Highways are toll-based (ticket at entry, payment at exit).

Don'ts

  • Sitting on the Spanish Steps (Trinità dei Monti) in Rome (€250 fine).
  • Bathing in fountains (Trevi, Quattro Fiumi…) — fine up to €450.
  • Eating/drinking on historical monument steps (Florence, Venice).
  • Love locks on bridges in Venice, Florence, Verona — fines.

3. Myths — what's true and what isn't

  • "Italians eat pasta every day" — partly true (high frequency), but not as a main meal. Often a small primo, then secondo (meat/fish) + contorno (vegetable).
  • "Italian = pizza and spaghetti" — false. Each region has its own cuisine. Risotto in the north, ragù in Bologna, seafood in coastal regions, polenta in the Alps.
  • "Italians are unpunctual" — partly true in the south, less so in the north. Trains run reasonably on time, business meetings less so.
  • "Italians don't drink water with meals" — false. They drink, but bottled (still/sparkling), not tap. Wine often only at dinner, not every day.
  • "Italian = chaos" — partly true in traffic and bureaucracy, but in family and work things have their order. Just a different one than ours.
  • "You should haggle in shops" — false. In normal stores prices are fixed. Markets yes, especially in the south.
  • "It's hot in Italy all year" — false. Northern winter is harsh (Po Valley fog, snow in the Alps). Even summer in the mountains can be cool.

4. Language corner — the minimum that opens doors

Italians always appreciate effort. Even a few greetings in Italian dramatically change your reception.

Greetings and courtesy

  • Buongiorno — good day · Buonasera — good evening
  • Per favore — please · Grazie — thank you · Prego — you're welcome
  • Scusi — excuse me (formal) · Scusa — sorry (informal)
  • Mi dispiace — I'm sorry · Va bene — OK / fine
  • Parla inglese? — Do you speak English?

In the restaurant

  • Il conto, per favore — the bill, please
  • Vorrei... — I'd like...
  • Senza... — without... (allergies, e.g. senza glutine)
  • Acqua naturale / frizzante — still / sparkling water
  • Un caffè — an espresso (just say "un caffè", they know)

Numbers and days

  • 1 uno · 2 due · 3 tre · 4 quattro · 5 cinque · 6 sei · 7 sette · 8 otto · 9 nove · 10 dieci
  • Lunedì (Mon), Martedì, Mercoledì, Giovedì, Venerdì, Sabato, Domenica

5. Coffee types — complete overview

Italian coffee is its own world. Wrong order = polite but knowing smile from the barista. A small guide so you don't stand out as a foreigner.

  • Caffè = espresso. Default. If you just say "un caffè", you get espresso.
  • Cappuccino = espresso + steamed milk + foam. Only morning (before 11–12).
  • Caffè macchiato = espresso "stained" with a touch of milk. OK any time.
  • Latte macchiato = warm milk "stained" with espresso. Mostly for children/breakfast.
  • Caffè americano = espresso diluted with hot water. Italians drink it rarely; for tourists.
  • Caffè corretto = espresso "corrected" with grappa, sambuca, or other liquor.
  • Caffè freddo / shakerato = iced espresso (summer drink).
  • Caffè decaffeinato = decaf. Just say "decaffeinato".

How to drink: mostly standing at the bar. Pay first at the cash register (cassa), take receipt to barista, drink at the bar. Sitting at a table = double price.

6. Safety — pickpockets, scams, how to protect yourself

Where to be most careful

  • Rome: bus lines around Termini, area around Colosseum, Trevi Fountain.
  • Naples: Spaccanapoli, central station, Piazza Garibaldi area at night.
  • Florence: around Ponte Vecchio and Uffizi.
  • Milan: Duomo area and Central Station.
  • Venice: Rialto, San Marco, water taxis.

Most common scams

  • "Free" bracelet / rose — someone ties a bracelet on or hands you a rose, then demands money. Just say "no grazie" and don't take it.
  • Stolen wallet trick — distraction (someone bumps into you / asks for directions / spills coffee), while accomplice steals wallet.
  • Fake police — "officers" ask to see your wallet "to check for counterfeit bills". Real police never does this. Say "ID please" — they leave.
  • Inflated restaurant bill — always read the bill before paying. Watch for coperto, servizio, items you didn't order.
  • Taxi without meter — only use taxis with meter (tassametro). Refuse "fixed price" deals; agree price in advance if no meter.

Practical advice

  • Carry money in front pockets or money belt under clothes. Backpack on chest in crowds.
  • Don't leave bag on chair back in cafés/restaurants — keep it on your lap or between feet.
  • Photo copies of documents (passport, insurance) in luggage + phone cloud.
  • Emergency number: 112 (universal EU) or 113 (police).
  • Travel insurance with theft cover — pays off for any longer trip.

Italy is generally a safe country for tourists. The main risk is petty theft and scams, not violence. Common sense + the rules above and you're sorted for 99% of cases.
