Italy isn't one cuisine

Italian cooking is strongly regional. Carbonara from Rome, ragù from Bologna, pesto from Genoa or risotto from Milan — each region has its own ingredients, rules and family variations. The goal of this page isn't to claim the one truth, but to show an honest foundation.

📌 Note

Italian cuisine may vary by region, town or family tradition. I present recipes as a verified traditional version from a specific source, not as the only existing possibility.

Where I draw from

  • Personal experience — from when I lived in Italy (Lerici, Tuscany), from everyday life with Italians and from traveling through different regions.
  • Verified Italian sources — Italia.it, Accademia Italiana della Cucina, official regional tourism sites, DOP/IGP/STG consortia.
  • Italian YouTube cooking channels — GialloZafferano and CookAround as audiovisual supplement, not as sole proof of tradition.
  • Regional differences — where a recipe exists in multiple variants, it's clearly labeled.

Spaghetti alla carbonara · Lazio / Roma

Spaghetti alla carbonara

🇮🇹 Lazioclassic versionhome version

Roman classic. Yellow pasta coated in a creamy bond of egg, hard sheep's cheese and guanciale fat. No cream — creaminess comes from yolk, cheese and pasta water.

Key ingredients

  • spaghetti (or rigatoni)
  • guanciale (traditionally, not pancetta and definitely not ham)
  • egg (yolks + one whole)
  • pecorino romano
  • black pepper

Regional note: the traditional Roman version uses no onion or garlic. Northern variants sometimes include pancetta but in Rome that's considered a swap.

Spaghetti all'amatriciana · Lazio / Amatrice

Spaghetti all'amatriciana

🇮🇹 Lazioclassic versionhome version

From the town of Amatrice in Sabina. The triad: guanciale, San Marzano tomatoes, pecorino di Amatrice. Traditionally without garlic or onion. Registered as STG (Specialità Tradizionale Garantita).

Key ingredients

  • spaghetti, rigatoni or bucatini
  • guanciale
  • peeled tomatoes (San Marzano)
  • pecorino romano (traditionally from Amatrice)
  • chili, dry white wine

Regional note: pancetta is sometimes accepted as substitute, but true amatriciana is with guanciale. STG specification registered in EU 2020.

📖 GialloZafferano · verify recipe

Tagliatelle al ragù alla bolognese · Emilia-Romagna / Bologna

Tagliatelle al ragù

🇮🇹 Emilia-Romagnaclassic versionhome version

Real bolognese is NOT served with spaghetti, but with tagliatelle (egg, wide). "Spaghetti bolognese" is a British-American adaptation. Official recipe registered with Accademia Italiana della Cucina in a notary act.

Key ragù ingredients

  • ground beef (optionally + pancetta)
  • onion, carrot, celery (soffritto)
  • tomato paste or passed tomatoes
  • dry white wine
  • milk (yes, milk — for smoothness)
  • tagliatelle, parmigiano reggiano

Regional note: in Bologna spaghetti bolognese isn't served in restaurants — anyone who orders them outs themselves as a tourist. Ragù can also be served with pappardelle.

📖 Accademia Italiana della Cucina · official

Gnocchi al pesto alla genovese · Liguria / Genova

Gnocchi al pesto

🇮🇹 Liguriaclassic versionhome version

Pesto alla genovese has 7 strictly defined ingredients per Consorzio del Pesto Genovese. Potato gnocchi are the traditional partner. Pancetta on top is a home variation.

Key pesto ingredients

  • basilico genovese DOP
  • extra virgin olive oil Riviera Ligure
  • parmigiano reggiano + fiore sardo (sheep)
  • pine nuts
  • garlic from Vessalico
  • coarse sea salt

Regional note: traditionally made in a marble mortar (grinding, not blending). Pancetta variation is a home choice, not a classic recipe.

📖 GialloZafferano · verify recipe

Risotto alla milanese · Lombardia / Milano

Risotto alla milanese

🇮🇹 Lombardiaclassic versionhome version

Milanese classic in bright yellow from saffron. Traditionally cooked on bone marrow and beef stock. Served as side to ossobuco or alone.

Key ingredients

  • Carnaroli or Arborio rice
  • saffron threads
  • bone marrow (traditional, can be skipped)
  • beef stock
  • parmigiano reggiano, butter, onion, dry white wine

Regional note: in Lombardy served "all'onda" — liquid but not soup. Plated flat on the plate.

📖 GialloZafferano · verify recipe

Risotto al pomodoro · all Italy

Risotto al pomodoro

🇮🇹 Italyclassic versionhome version

Simple summer risotto with tomatoes. Not a classified regional specialty but part of home cuisine across Italy. Soffritto, passed tomatoes, parmigiano.

Key ingredients

  • Carnaroli/Arborio rice
  • passata di pomodoro
  • cherry tomatoes (optional)
  • onion, garlic, basil
  • parmigiano, butter, olive oil

Regional note: variations with mozzarella or pancetta in different regions.

📖 GialloZafferano · verify recipe

Spaghetti al pomodoro · all Italy

Spaghetti al pomodoro

🇮🇹 Italymy variationhome version

The most basic Italian pasta. Three ingredients: tomatoes, basil, olive oil. Symbol of Italian simplicity — quality of ingredients over complexity of technique.

Key ingredients

  • spaghetti
  • quality pomidoro/passata (San Marzano)
  • extra virgin olive oil
  • garlic, fresh basil
  • parmigiano (optional)

Regional note: in the south (Campania, Sicily) no cheese. In the north occasionally parmigiano or pecorino added.

📖 GialloZafferano · verify recipe

Penne al pomodoro / arrabbiata · all Italy

Penne al pomodoro

🇮🇹 Italymy variationhome version

Penne with tomato sauce. If with chili, it becomes penne all'arrabbiata (Lazio). Penne (rigate) shape holds the sauce in its grooves.

Key ingredients

  • penne rigate
  • peeled tomatoes
  • garlic, olive oil
  • chili (for arrabbiata)
  • parsley, pecorino

Regional note: arrabbiata means "angry" — because of chili. Roman classic.

📖 GialloZafferano · verify recipe

Penne alla panna · Northern Italy

Penne alla panna

🇮🇹 Northmy variationhome version

Creamy pasta from northern Italy (Lombardy, Piedmont, Veneto). Often with ham (prosciutto cotto) or mushrooms. Watch out: cream in carbonara = mistake, but in penne alla panna = legitimate.

Key ingredients

  • penne
  • cream (panna da cucina)
  • parmigiano
  • prosciutto cotto (optional)
  • black pepper, nutmeg

Regional note: this is the north's answer to roman carbonara. Different region, different rules.

🌾 Italian pasta types and what they suit

13 main shapes you'll encounter. Each is paired with sauces it best holds.

  • Spaghetti — long thin. Tomato sauces, carbonara, aglio e olio.
  • Spaghettoni — thicker spaghetti. Heavier sauces.
  • Linguine — flat ribbons. Seafood, pesto.
  • Fettuccine / Tagliatelle — wide egg ribbons. Ragù, butter sauces.
  • Pappardelle — wide ribbons. Hearty meat ragùs (wild boar, hare).
  • Penne (rigate) — diagonally cut tubes with ridges. Tomato sauces, arrabbiata.
  • Rigatoni — wide ridged tubes. Carbonara north variant, amatriciana.
  • Fusilli — corkscrews. Cold pasta salads, pesto.
  • Farfalle — bow ties. Cream sauces, salads.
  • Orecchiette — "little ears". Apulian classic with broccoli rabe.
  • Bucatini — spaghetti with a hole. Amatriciana.
  • Conchiglie — shells. Holds chunky sauces.
  • Gnocchi — potato dumplings. Pesto, butter and sage, light tomato.

Rule of thumb: thin pasta with light sauces, thick/ridged pasta with heavy sauces.

🌾 How to recognize good pasta and honest ingredients

  • Bronze-die pasta (trafilata al bronzo) — rougher surface holds sauce. Look for "trafilata al bronzo" on the label.
  • Slow drying at low temp — better starch structure. Premium brands list "essiccata lentamente".
  • Italian wheat — "grano italiano" matters for some. Most pasta uses imported wheat (Canada, Australia) — not necessarily worse.
  • DOP/IGP marks — guarantee of origin and method. Examples: Pasta di Gragnano IGP, Pomodoro San Marzano DOP, Pesto Genovese, Parmigiano Reggiano DOP.
  • Olive oil — extra virgin (EVOO), cold pressed, "Italian" not "bottled in Italy". Check origin on label.
  • Cheese: parmigiano reggiano (cow, north), pecorino romano (sheep, Lazio), grana padano (cow, milder). Each has specific use.

⚠️ Common myths about Italian cuisine

  1. Carbonara has cream. No. Egg, pecorino, guanciale fat, pasta water = creaminess.
  2. Spaghetti bolognese is Italian. No. Bolognese ragù goes on tagliatelle. Spaghetti bolognese is a British-American invention.
  3. Pesto must be made in a blender. No. Traditional pesto is made in a marble mortar with wooden pestle. Blender heats the oil and oxidizes the basil.
  4. Italians eat pizza with knife and fork everywhere. Depends. Restaurant pizza, especially Neapolitan, often eaten with hands (folded "a libretto"). Knife/fork in formal settings.
  5. Pineapple on pizza is a global thing. No. In Italy "Hawaiian" pizza is considered an insult to pizza culture. Don't order it there.
  6. Pasta cooked al dente means undercooked. No. Al dente means firm to the bite, not raw. Cooking pasta soft = mushy, sauce won't stick.
  7. Garlic is in every Italian recipe. No. Many classics use no garlic (carbonara, amatriciana, alfredo). South uses more garlic than north.
  8. Parmesan on everything. No. Never on seafood pasta. Never on fish. The fundamental rule of Italian cooking.

🗺 Regional differences

Brief overview of regional cuisine characters:

  • North (Lombardia, Piemonte, Veneto): butter, cream, polenta, risotto, ossobuco, gorgonzola.
  • Center (Toscana, Lazio, Umbria): olive oil, beans, bread soup (ribollita), wild game, pecorino.
  • South (Campania, Calabria, Puglia): tomato base, seafood, mozzarella di bufala, chili, dried pasta.
  • Islands (Sicilia, Sardegna): seafood, citrus, sweet/savory mix (pasta alla Norma, arancini, pane carasau).
  • Mountains (Trentino, Friuli): Austrian/Slovenian influences. Speck, knödel, sauerkraut variants.

The further south, the more tomatoes, olive oil and chili. The further north, the more butter, rice and meat broths.
