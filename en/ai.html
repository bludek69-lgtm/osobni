For me, AI isn't a magic black box or a replacement for thinking. It's a partner that helps me write, plan, find bugs, build websites, run my smart home and orient myself in complex topics.

I treat AI as a smart work tool. Something like a mix of notebook, consultant, proofreader, programmer and that annoying colleague who keeps asking "do you really mean that?" Used well, it saves time and helps me work more systematically. Used badly, it just quickly produces chaos.

Vibe coding: when I describe an idea in plain language and AI helps with code

Vibe coding means a person describes what they want to build and AI helps design or write the code. It does NOT mean blindly accepting whatever AI generates. In my projects it's important: first the idea, then the design, then the review, then the test, and only then deployment.

I don't treat it as "AI builds the app and I just click". I treat it as collaboration: I set direction, AI proposes solutions, then we test, fix and guard safety. AI helps me turn ideas into code — but decisions, review and final deploy stay with me.
1AI helps turn ideas into code. I don't have to write everything from scratch.
2AI can design structure. Before I start, it's good to see how it could look.
3AI can find bugs in existing code. It often sees what I miss.
4AI can produce a first version quickly. But it's a first version, not final.
5A human must review, test and decide. Without that, vibe coding is chaos.
6For sensitive things (finance, home, data) I insist on a rollback plan. If something breaks, I must be able to revert.

Why AI often doesn't work for people

It's not always AI's fault. Mostly it's about how you talk to it.

1. They ask too generally

"Write me something about Italy" leads nowhere. AI doesn't know who it's writing for, how long, or what interests you. The more specific the question, the better the answer.

2. They don't give context

AI doesn't know who you are, what you do, who it's for, or what the result should be. A few sentences of context solve most problems.

3. They want a finished result without review

AI can help, but the result needs to be read — especially for finance, health, law, tech and smart homes.

4. They don't specify output format

There's a difference between wanting a table, an article, a prompt, a checklist, a plan or a tech procedure. If you don't say, AI guesses.

5. They don't continue the dialogue

AI works best as a conversation. The first answer is often just the start — a follow-up "now shorten it / focus on X / add examples" massively raises quality.

6. They believe everything

AI can sound confident even when wrong. So facts need verification — especially numbers, dates, names and quotes.

How to ask AI better

I have a simple formula that works for 90% of cases.

Example — web
You are an experienced personal website editor. Help me write a page about traveling to Italy for ordinary readers. The text should be human, practical, free of jargon. Output as a structure of headings, paragraphs and CTA buttons.
Example — smart home
You are a senior smart home architect. First do an audit, change nothing without my GO. Find risks, propose a safe procedure, prepare rollback and only then recommend the next step.

Where I'm careful

AI can't vouch for truth. It doesn't always see the current state of the world unless it has data access. It doesn't know your files unless you give them to it. It can confuse links. For important decisions, human review is needed.

!Vibe coding can create working but unsafe code. First versions often miss edge cases and security.
!AI can overlook security flaws. Especially around auth, encryption and permissions.
!AI can accidentally suggest exposing private data. Tokens, keys, emails, paths — review everything before commit.
!For finance, taxes, health, law and electricity, human review is mandatory. AI is a tool, not a responsible expert.
!For the web I check what goes public. No internal URLs, no tokens, no sensitive screenshots.
The biggest mistake isn't using AI. The biggest mistake is not checking what you let through to the world.
Be especially careful with: finance · health · law · security · electricity · home automation · personal data.

My approach

I use AI gradually. First the idea, then the design, then the review, then the test. For important things, I want a report, rollback plan and clear confirmation before anything is deployed. Thanks to that, AI doesn't become chaos, it becomes a tool that helps keep order.

I use this approach for smart home, web and finance. Less surprise, more control. AI helps me do each step faster, but I still make the decisions myself.

AI isn't just for programmers. It can help anyone who wants to write better, plan, learn, find errors or organize information. The most important thing isn't knowing all the tools. The most important thing is asking well.
